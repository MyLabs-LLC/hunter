# RA treatment web-evidence review

**Review date:** 2026-07-21  
**Scope:** current clinical guidance, comparative trials, systematic evidence, and study registries, following exhaustion of the 87-paper arXiv source.  
**Purpose:** research triage for `CASE-RA-D2T-A`; not medical advice, a treatment prescription, or evidence that a particular treatment will work for one person.

## Bottom line

This review found a research-supported *decision strategy*, not a patient-specific drug answer. For difficult-to-treat (D2T) RA, current guidance first requires confirming that persistent symptoms reflect active inflammatory disease rather than an alternative or coexisting cause. If inflammatory RA remains active after a DMARD failure, guidance supports changing to another biologic or targeted-synthetic DMARD; after a first TNF-inhibitor failure, comparative evidence favours changing to a different mechanism of action in the studied populations.

That does **not** prove that a non-TNF agent, a JAK inhibitor, or any specific medicine will work in this case. The available case input lacks the treatment history, objective inflammatory measurements, contraindication screen, and patient preferences needed to turn the strategy into a treatment decision. The existing local models also cannot supply those missing facts.

## What the evidence supports

### 1. Verify the target before escalating RA treatment

The D2T guidance says to establish whether inflammation is present before adjusting DMARD therapy. It specifically notes that non-inflammatory pain, fibromyalgia, osteoarthritis, structural damage, infection, malignancy, comorbidity, psychosocial factors, and non-adherence can make a case appear treatment-refractory. In the absence of active inflammation, further DMARD escalation is unlikely to help and can add risk. This is the first evidence-supported fork for this case, not a procedural formality.

The current EULAR recommendations retain a treat-to-target approach: aim for a substantial reduction in disease activity by three months and the agreed target by six months. They endorse short-term glucocorticoids only as a bridge, with rapid taper/discontinuation; persistent need for glucocorticoids should prompt a DMARD-strategy reassessment rather than become a long-term substitute.

### 2. If inflammatory RA is active, a mechanism change is supported after a TNFi failure

EULAR recommends another biologic or targeted-synthetic DMARD after failure of a biologic/targeted treatment. After one TNF- or IL-6-receptor inhibitor fails, it permits either a different mechanism or another drug in the same class, but says not to use the same class a third time. This is a sequence-level recommendation; it does not select a drug for a person.

In the 300-person trial of patients with persistent active RA after one TNFi, 69% assigned to a non-TNF biologic and 52% assigned to a second TNFi achieved a good or moderate EULAR response at 24 weeks (odds ratio 2.06, 95% CI 1.27–3.37). The low-disease-activity proportion was also higher with the non-TNF strategy (45% vs 28% at 24 weeks). The study was open label, and its result should not be extrapolated to people who stopped a TNFi for a different reason, never had objective inflammation, or have important overlapping disease.

The subsequent network meta-analysis was directionally consistent: switching to a different mechanism was more likely than TNFi cycling to achieve ACR50 and less likely to lead to treatment withdrawal in its fixed-effect analysis, while random-effects estimates were less certain. It supports the strategy but does not identify a universally best next medicine.

### 3. A recent JAK-inhibitor trial is a signal, not a solution for this case

SELECT-SWITCH randomized 492 methotrexate-treated patients who had inadequate response or intolerance to one non-adalimumab TNFi. At week 12, the upadacitinib group had more DAS28-CRP low disease activity than the adalimumab group (43.3% vs 22.4%; difference 21.0 percentage points, 95% CI 12.9–29.1) and more ACR50 responses (38.2% vs 26.8%). These results strengthen the general case for a mechanism switch in that trial population.

They are not sufficient to name upadacitinib as this case's treatment. The FDA requires boxed warnings for JAK inhibitors used in inflammatory conditions, including risks of serious heart-related events, cancer, blood clots, and death. The regulator directs clinicians to weigh individual benefits and risks, particularly for people who smoke or have cardiovascular risk factors, current or prior malignancy, or other relevant risks. The case record includes an unresolved cardiac workup and is otherwise incomplete for this safety screen.

### 4. Combination, monotherapy, monitoring, and remission are not interchangeable claims

EULAR generally recommends combining a biologic or targeted-synthetic DMARD with a conventional synthetic DMARD. When a conventional synthetic DMARD cannot be used, IL-6-pathway inhibitors and JAK inhibitors may have relative advantages as monotherapy. Routine therapeutic drug monitoring is not recommended because the cited trial did not show better outcomes than routine care. In sustained remission, dose reduction may be considered, whereas stopping DMARDs leads to flares in most patients. None of these population statements establishes that one of those situations applies here.

## What remains unknown for `CASE-RA-D2T-A`

The available research cannot resolve these treatment-determinative questions:

- Is there active synovitis/inflammation now (joint examination, validated activity score, CRP/ESR, and imaging where clinically indicated), versus pain or impairment with another driver?
- Was certolizumab ineffective, intolerable, interrupted for access/adherence, stopped for safety, or insufficiently dosed/durable? What happened with every prior csDMARD, TNFi, IL-6R inhibitor, B-cell therapy, co-stimulation therapy, and targeted-synthetic DMARD?
- What are RF/anti-CCP status, radiographic progression, infection history, tuberculosis/hepatitis screening, blood counts/liver/kidney status, lung disease, malignancy history, thrombotic risk, smoking history, and cardiovascular status?
- How should the IBD/autoimmune-overlap phenotype and its current treatment constrain RA choices? This requires coordinated specialty assessment.
- What outcomes and trade-offs matter to the patient (disease control, pain, fatigue, route, pregnancy plans if relevant, adverse-event tolerance, and access)?

These are clinical data gaps, not parameters that a local RNA-seq classifier can validly infer. The fixed GSE129705 model remains exploratory (external ROC-AUC 0.662; bootstrap interval 0.443–0.851) and must not select or rule out a drug.

## Investigational and negative findings

The registry lists IMVT-1402 trial NCT06754462 as active but not recruiting, with no posted results. It therefore offers no efficacy evidence and no current local experimental dataset. The previously completed arXiv loop also found no new public baseline RA treatment-response cohort compatible with the sealed local evaluator. No additional model run was justified: substituting this clinical literature for a labeled dataset would not produce a valid experiment.

## Research conclusion and stop condition

This web tranche read current guidance, a directly relevant D2T framework, a comparative randomized trial, a network meta-analysis, a recent specific mechanism-switch trial, regulator safety information, and a current trial registry record. It reached **no patient-level treatment proof**.

The strongest defensible finding is conditional: *if* a clinician confirms active inflammatory RA and documents an inadequate response to a first TNFi, then a change to a different mechanism has stronger comparative support than automatically cycling to a second TNFi in the studied populations. Choosing the actual therapy requires the unresolved case variables and individualized safety assessment above. The evidence loop therefore stops `no_individualizable_treatment_evidence` rather than pretending that additional web reading can close a missing clinical-data contract.
